Modeling the Costs of Incorrect Demand Predictions

Ethan Shaw BYU–Idaho · Data Science
June 2026 5 min read

A demand forecast is only useful if it improves a decision. In e-commerce, the most important question is often not whether the forecast was mathematically perfect. It is whether the forecast helped the business avoid expensive inventory mistakes.

Incorrect predictions usually create two different kinds of cost. If the model underpredicts demand, the business may run out of inventory and lose sales. If the model overpredicts demand, the business may buy too much inventory and trap cash in products that move slowly. Both errors matter, but they do not always cost the same.

Forecast Error Is Not Just a Number

Common model metrics like MAE, RMSE, or MAPE are useful, but they do not fully describe the business impact. A model that is off by 20 units on a high-margin best seller may be much more expensive than a model that is off by 20 units on a slow, low-margin product.

That is why I like thinking about demand prediction as a cost model. Instead of only asking, "How wrong was the forecast?", I want to ask, "What did that error cause the business to do?"

The Cost of Underprediction

Underprediction happens when actual demand is higher than forecasted demand. The most obvious cost is lost revenue from stockouts. If a store sells out of a product while customers still want it, every missed order is a direct revenue opportunity that disappeared.

But stockouts can also create softer costs. Customers may switch to a competitor. Advertising spend may become less efficient because traffic is landing on unavailable products. A marketplace listing may lose momentum if sales velocity drops. The product may even lose ranking in search results.

A basic underprediction cost can be estimated like this:

Stockout cost = missed units x contribution margin per unit

In a more advanced model, I would also consider customer lifetime value, marketplace ranking effects, and the time required to recover after inventory arrives again.

The Cost of Overprediction

Overprediction happens when the forecast expects more demand than the business actually receives. This can lead to excess inventory. That inventory may still have value, but it creates a cash flow problem because money is locked inside products instead of being available for marketing, payroll, new product tests, or operations.

Overprediction can also create storage costs, discounting pressure, product obsolescence, and cluttered decision-making. When a small business has too many slow movers, it becomes harder to know which products deserve attention.

A basic overprediction cost can be estimated like this:

Excess cost = extra units x holding cost per unit

If products are seasonal or trend-driven, the holding cost should be higher because the value of the product may fall over time.

Why Asymmetric Costs Matter

In many cases, being too low and being too high are not equally bad. For a product with strong margins and reliable demand, underpredicting might be worse because missed sales are painful. For a product with uncertain demand or high storage costs, overpredicting might be worse because excess inventory creates risk.

This is where service levels and safety stock become important. A business may choose to hold more safety stock for products where stockouts are expensive and less safety stock for products where excess inventory is the bigger danger.

The Goal Is Better Decisions

The most useful demand model is not always the one with the lowest technical error. It is the one that helps the business make better purchasing decisions. That means evaluating models in the language of the business: missed revenue, trapped cash, reorder timing, and risk.

When forecasting is connected to cost modeling, it becomes easier to choose the right model, set the right safety stock, and explain why a reorder recommendation matters.
