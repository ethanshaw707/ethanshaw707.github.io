Using Machine Learning to Improve Inventory Forecasting for E-Commerce Businesses

Ethan Shaw BYU–Idaho · Data Science
April 2026 6 min read

For my senior project, I wanted to choose something that connects directly to my interests, my degree, and the business work I am already doing. I am currently in my final semester at BYU-Idaho, studying data science, and I am especially interested in machine learning, advanced analytics, artificial intelligence, and how these tools can solve real business problems.

Over the past several months, I have been working on a business with a friend that helps e-commerce businesses improve their operations. Through that experience, I have seen that one of the biggest challenges many online businesses face is inventory planning. Ordering too much inventory can tie up cash and create storage problems. Ordering too little inventory can lead to stockouts, missed sales, unhappy customers, and slower growth.

This problem becomes even more difficult because many e-commerce businesses order inventory from overseas suppliers, often from China. That means inventory may take two or three months to arrive after it is ordered. Because of that long lead time, businesses cannot simply wait until they are almost out of stock to place another order. They need to predict future demand early enough to make good purchasing decisions. My senior project is focused on whether machine learning and data science forecasting methods can improve SKU-level demand prediction and help e-commerce businesses know when to reorder inventory.

The Problem: Inventory Decisions Are Expensive to Get Wrong

In e-commerce, each product usually has a SKU, which stands for stock keeping unit. A SKU is basically an identification code for a specific product or variation of a product. For example, if a store sells a candle in three sizes and five scents, each combination may have a different SKU.

The challenge is that every SKU may have a different demand pattern. Some products sell consistently every week. Others sell heavily during certain seasons. Some products may sell randomly, with long periods of no sales followed by sudden spikes. Because of this, it is not enough to simply look at total store sales. Businesses need to understand demand at the SKU level.

If a business guesses wrong, the consequences can be serious. If they order too much, they may have thousands of dollars stuck in inventory that does not move. If they order too little, they may run out of a popular product and lose revenue. For small businesses, either mistake can be painful.

This is why I believe inventory forecasting is a valuable problem to study. It combines data science, business strategy, and real-world decision-making.

My Project Goal

The main question I want to answer is:

Will machine learning and time-series forecasting methods improve SKU-level demand prediction and help e-commerce businesses make better inventory purchasing decisions?

To explore this question, I plan to build a Python package that takes historical order data, creates demand forecasts, and generates reorder recommendations. The package will be designed to help businesses estimate when they should reorder a product before they run out of stock.

The final output of the project will focus on a simple but important decision: Should this product be reordered now?

In the project, this will be represented as a binary prediction:

  • 1 = Yes, reorder
  • 0 = No, do not reorder yet

This makes the project practical because the goal is not just to create a forecast chart. The goal is to create a tool that can help a business make a real decision.

What I Plan to Build

The main deliverable for this project will be a Python package. This package will take raw order history data and turn it into useful forecasting and inventory recommendations. The package will include several major steps.

01

Ingest and clean raw order data

E-commerce data can be messy, especially when it comes from different platforms or clients. The data will need to be organized into time-series datasets, where each SKU has its own sales history over time.

02

Engineer features that capture demand patterns

Lag variables, rolling averages, seasonal indicators, month-over-month changes. A model may need to know how many units sold last week, the average sold over the past month, or whether the product tends to sell more in a certain season.

03

Test multiple forecasting methods

I want to compare traditional statistical forecasting methods with machine learning models like random forest and gradient boosting, and see which methods perform best for different types of products.

04

Classify products by demand behavior

Some products have smooth demand. Others are erratic or intermittent. One forecasting model may work well for steady-demand SKUs but perform poorly on ones that only sell occasionally.

05

Calculate inventory metrics

Reorder points, safety stock, and stockout probability — the metrics that translate a forecast into an actual business recommendation.

Why This Project Matters

This project matters because it addresses a real problem that many e-commerce businesses face. Inventory forecasting is not just a technical problem. It directly affects cash flow, customer satisfaction, fulfillment speed, and business growth.

For small businesses especially, better forecasting can make a major difference. A large company may be able to absorb inventory mistakes more easily, but a small business may not have that same flexibility. If a small business spends too much money on the wrong inventory, it may limit its ability to invest in marketing, new products, or better operations.

At the same time, running out of inventory can be just as damaging. If a product is selling well and the business runs out, customers may buy from competitors instead. The business loses revenue and may lose repeat customers.

By using data science and machine learning, I hope to create a tool that helps businesses make smarter decisions before inventory problems happen.

What I Hope to Learn

Through this project, I hope to strengthen my understanding of time-series forecasting, machine learning, and inventory analytics. I also want to learn more about best practices in demand forecasting, including safety stock, reorder points, and how to handle seasonal or intermittent demand.

This project will also help me connect my data science education to the real business problems I want to solve after college. Instead of building a project only for a class, I want this senior project to become something that could eventually be used as a feature in client-facing software.

Conclusion

My senior project will focus on building a machine learning and data science forecasting package for e-commerce inventory planning. The goal is to help businesses predict SKU-level demand and make better reorder decisions.

The project will involve cleaning order data, creating time-series features, comparing forecasting models, classifying demand patterns, and calculating inventory metrics. Ultimately, I want to create a practical tool that helps answer a simple but important question:

Should this product be reordered now?

I am excited about this project because it connects my interests in machine learning, analytics, AI, and entrepreneurship. It also gives me the opportunity to solve a real problem that affects e-commerce businesses every day.
